Key Learning
On small datasets (n=2,781), CV AUC and leaderboard AUC can diverge significantly. More features do not always generalise better. The best model is often the simplest well-regularised one.
Key Findings
01
Speed is relative, not absolute
Sprint_40yd_pos_z (rank 1) outranks raw Sprint_40yd (rank 12) by 4x. Being faster than your positional peers is what NFL scouts measure — not raw speed versus all positions.
02
School prestige is a real signal
School_encoded ranks #2. Georgia, Oklahoma, Miami (FL), Clemson, and Ohio St. all produce 73–80% draft rates, far above the 65% average. Elite programs are genuine predictors.
03
Missing data tells a story
Age_missing ranks #3 — above all raw performance metrics. Athletes who skip age reporting are not randomly distributed. The absence of data is itself a meaningful predictor of draft outcome.
04
Position determines the baseline
A 53-point draft rate gap separates OLBs (75.9%) from Punters (22.7%). Position_rate (#8) encodes this prior directly, giving the model a strong structural baseline per athlete type.
05
CV overfitting is a real risk
Submission 2 (49 features, CV 0.857) scored worse on the public leaderboard than submission 1 (33 features, CV 0.852). On n=2,781, every additional feature must earn its place.
06
Top leaderboard scores are suspicious
Public AUC scores of 1.0, 0.9999, and 0.9998 are statistically implausible through legitimate modelling on real NFL data. The private leaderboard, scored after submission cutoff, will likely reorder results significantly.
